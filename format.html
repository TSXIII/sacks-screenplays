Formatting a Script

Now that you know the correct way to structure a plot, let's discuss formatting a script. A screenplay's actual content will vary based on one's writing style and story, but there are specific terms authors use to correctly articulate their ideas:

Term How it's Used
Beat Tells the reader a character is pausing while speaking or doing something.
CLOSE ON or INSERT Tells the reader a camera shot is emphasizing a particular item.
CONTINUOUS Tells the reader a scene takes place in multiple locations without a change in time.
CUT TO Tells the reader a scene is jumping from one to the next without a smooth transition.
DISSOLVE TO Tells the reader a scene will transition from one to the next via a smooth shot dissolve.
ESTABLISHING SHOT Tells the reader a scene begins with a distant shot of a particular location.
EXT. Tells the reader a scene takes place outside.
FADE TO Tells the reader a scene will transition from one to the next via a fade to black.
INT. Tells the reader a scene takes place inside.
INTERCUT BETWEEN Tells the reader multiple scenes are happening simultaneously and their events are all being described at once.
INTO FRAME Tells the reader the camera is emphasizing a particular character's entrance.
JUMP CUT TO Tells the reader a rough edit will be made in which the camera only moves slightly from one shot to another to indicate the passage of time.
MATCH CUT TO Tells the reader an edit will be made in which the camera moves from a shot of the character taking one action to another of the character taking a similar one.
MONTAGE Tells the reader a series of shots will depict a character's completion of several tasks in sequential order.
O.S. or O.C. Tells the reader a speaking character is not on-camera when delivering his or her dialogue.
POV Tells the reader a shot will depict the events of a scene from a particular character's perspective.
SMASH CUT TO Tells the reader a very abrupt cut is to be made from one scene to another.
SUPER, SUPER TITLE, or TITLE Tells the reader words will appear on the screen.
TIGHT ON Tells the reader there is to be a close-up shot of a particular character or object in a scene.
V.O. Tells the reader a character will be providing offscreen narration or only contributing vocally to a scene.

This may seem like a lot to digest, but remember these terms need only appear in a script as they are needed. The idea is to clearly and concisely communicate a scene's flow to those who look at your screenplay.

When writing your script, make sure you start each scene with a heading that includes "INT." or "EXT." and is followed by the respective location and time of day in which it takes place. After your heading, place any action (descriptions of what a character sees, hears, or does) or dialogue (what a character says). When introducing a character for the first time, write his/her name in capital letters. If you envision a scene ending a particular way, such as jumping to another place or simply changing to the next scene via a dissolve, indicate this on the right side of the page. Make sure you only write what the audience can see or hear when watching a finished film; there is no need to describe a character's feelings since this is implied through visible emotion and vocal tone onscreen.

PRO TIP

Use a free writing tool like Celtx or Beat to make formatting your screenplay a little easier. These platforms do it automatically.
